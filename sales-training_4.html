Four modules. One flow. Master the introduction, value, price, and close — and you have everything you need to go out and produce.

01

The Introduction

You have about 10 seconds. Your intro needs to be natural, confident, and loaded with reasons for them to keep listening. Here's exactly how it flows.

Script The Full Introduction
Hey, how's it going?

My name's [Name] — I'm with [Service]. So I've got about 6 or 7 of your neighbors I'm taking care of tomorrow...

Real quick — it's been a lot of these right up here in the eaves, and tons of cobwebs and spiders just on the back patio.

And the nice thing is, since my truck's already gonna be here today and tomorrow — you guys just get that same flat rate we're doing for the neighborhood.
Notice what's in this intro: social proof (neighbors), a specific pain point (eaves, cobwebs, spiders), and a built-in reason to act now (truck's already here, flat rate). All in under 20 seconds.
Breakdown Why This Intro Works
  • 1
    Social proof up front. "6 or 7 of your neighbors" — you're already in the neighborhood, you're not a stranger, you're the guy everyone else is using.
  • 2
    Specific pain point. Don't say "bugs in general." Say the eaves. Say cobwebs on the back patio. Specific = believable. Generic = ignored.
  • 3
    Built-in scarcity. The truck being there today and tomorrow is real urgency. The flat neighborhood rate makes them feel like they'd be missing out to say no.
Assumptive language starts here. Don't say "if you were interested..." — say "since my truck's already gonna be here." You're not asking for a meeting. You're telling them what's happening.
The Fork Buying Question vs. Objection — Two Paths

After your intro, one of two things happens. Either they show interest — that's a buying question — or they push back. Here's how to read each one and where to go.

✓ BUYING QUESTION
They ask: "How much is it?" or "What do you guys do exactly?" or "How often do you come out?"

This is a green light. They're interested. Join their team — get right next to them — and walk them through value, price, and close.
✕ OBJECTION
They say: "We already have someone," "We're good," or "Not interested."

This is not rejection — it's a speed bump. Neutralize it and get back on the road. See the objection script below.
Script Handling the Objection — "We Already Have Someone"

The three most common objections you'll hear are: "We already have someone," "We're good," and "Not interested." They all get handled the same way.

THEY SAY: "We already have someone." / "We're good." / "Not interested."

No worries at all — who's taking care of you guys?

THEY NAME THE COMPANY.

Oh perfect. [Smile.] Awesome — great company.

I'm sure you've seen them treat the home before, right? Do the foundation, the inside — and probably give you a wipe-down if you see anything?

THEY SAY YES.

Perfect. Is there anything extra you're paying for on top of that, or is that pretty much it?
Validate the competition. Never trash them. "Great company" disarms defensiveness immediately — now you're on the same team. You're curious, not competing.
Then go right into it
So the real beauty of why a lot of your neighbors are giving us a shot — and honestly why they're upgrading — is that I don't just do the foundation and perimeter of the home.

I treat all the [ants / spiders / whatever the neighborhood pain point is] — starting from the yard all the way through.

→ From here, move into Value.
Pick the specific bug that's the pain of that neighborhood and name it. "Ants and spiders" is fine as a default — but if you know it's been scorpions or wasps in that area, say that. Specific pain = real credibility.
02

Building Value

Before price ever comes up, they need to feel the difference. Stack your value points one at a time, check in after each one, and make sure they're with you.

Framework Join Their Team First

Whether they came in through a buying question or you just handled an objection — once you're in, get physically next to them. Side by side, not face to face. You're on their team now, showing them something together.

Join their side
→
Value 1 — Yard/Perimeter
→
Check in
→
Value 2 — Eaves
→
Check in
→
Price
Script Value 1 — Full Yard & Perimeter
So I don't just do the foundation and perimeter of the home — I start at the front curb and treat the entire yard, all the way to the back fence.

Because here's the thing — if you're not treating the yard where the bugs actually live and breed, they're just gonna keep making their way up into the walls no matter what you spray inside.

Does that make sense? Pretty cool, right?
The check-in — "Does that make sense? Pretty cool, right?" — is non-negotiable. It keeps them engaged and gets a small yes before you move to the next point. Small yeses lead to the big yes.
Script Value 2 — Eaves & Roofline
Something else I do for you guys every single service — I take a large 30-foot brush and de-web the full exterior of the house. Windows, doors included.

That way a lot of those mud daubers and spiders don't keep laying their eggs and nesting on the home.

That's important because if you don't stop them there, they're just gonna keep getting inside.
Point up at the eaves when you say this. Physical gesture makes it real — they look up, they see the cobwebs, and you've just shown them their problem and your solution at the same time.
Assumptive Language Sound Like the Sale Is Already Happening

Every word during value-building should assume they're already a customer. No "ifs." No "if you were to sign up." Just forward momentum.

Weak
"If you were to sign up, my guy would come out and treat..."
Assumptive
"When my guy comes out, he's gonna treat your entire yard..."
Weak
"If this sounds good to you, we could get you on the schedule..."
Assumptive
"So once we get you on the schedule, here's what's gonna happen..."
03

Delivering Price

Price is just a number. Delivered the right way — after value is built, with scarcity and a warranty — it lands like a deal, not a demand.

Framework The Price Delivery Formula
Anchor High
→
Warranty
→
Scarcity Drop
→
Close

Always say the full price first. Then the warranty. Then drop to the neighborhood rate. In that order, every time.

Script The Price Delivery
Perfect. So if you were to call our office or go online, the initial would normally be $399 — not gonna charge you that. After that it's just $179 every two months.

But since my truck's already gonna be here — I'm just gonna take $300 off. So it's just $99 for everything.

And the cool thing is — if you see any bugs between those two months, we're coming back same day or next day. Free.
Let the $99 land. Don't rush past it. The contrast between $399 and $99 does the selling for you — pause, let it sit, then go straight into the route close.
Breakdown Why the Warranty Matters

The warranty isn't just a feature — it's a risk remover. Most people don't buy because they're afraid they'll spend money and it won't work. The warranty neutralizes that fear completely.

  • 1
    It shifts the risk onto you, not them
  • 2
    It signals confidence — you'd only offer a guarantee if you knew the service worked
  • 3
    It removes the "what if" objection before they can even raise it
Don't breeze past the warranty. Slow down when you say it. "I guarantee it" delivered with certainty carries enormous weight. Rushing it throws away one of your best tools.
04

The Close

Right after price — no fluff, no long wind-up. Show them the opening, give them a specific time, and let the assumptive close do the work.

Script The Route Close
Perfect. Let me show you this —

I do have this opening after the Johnsons at 4 PM. Does that work for you?
That's it. You're not asking if they want to buy — you're asking if 4 PM works. The decision has already been made. All they're doing now is confirming the time.
Breakdown Why This Close Works
  • 1
    "Let me show you this" — pulls their attention in. Creates a micro moment of curiosity right before the ask.
  • 2
    "After the Johnsons" — real neighbor, real schedule. This isn't a vague offer. It's a specific slot that actually exists. Social proof and scarcity in four words.
  • 3
    "Does that work for you?" — you're not asking if they want to buy. You're asking if the time works. The sale is assumed. The only question left is logistics.
After you say "Does that work for you?" — stop. Don't add anything. Don't soften it. Hold eye contact and wait. The first person to speak loses.
Assumptive Language At the Close

The route close is already fully assumptive — but here's the contrast so reps can hear the difference clearly.

Hesitant
"So... do you think you'd want to go ahead and sign up today?"
Assumptive
"I've got an opening after the Johnsons at 4 PM — does that work for you?"
Hesitant
"Would you be okay with me scheduling something?"
Assumptive
"Let me get your name and address and we'll get you locked in."
Script If They Hesitate — Back Half Entry

If they say "I need to think about it" or "let me talk to my spouse" — don't panic. Don't walk. This is where the sale actually starts.

Hey, totally understand. But let me just ask you real quick — money aside for a second — does the overall idea of what I'm saying make sense to you? Like do you at least like the idea of what we're doing?

THEY SAY YES.

Of course it does — because it just makes sense, right? So let me just show you one more thing real quick...

→ Add another value point, then route close again.
Get them to agree the idea makes sense — that's the reset. It moves them from "I'm not sure" back to "I actually agree with you." Then you route close again, same as before.
